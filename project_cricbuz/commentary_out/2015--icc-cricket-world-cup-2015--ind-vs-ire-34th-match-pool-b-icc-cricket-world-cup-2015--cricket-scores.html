Tied on points with South Africa, who thrashed them, and Pakistan, who they play their final league match against. Desperate to make the quarter-finals.Match 34 of the 2015 ICC Cricket World Cup pits these two teams together at Seddon Park on Tuesday, with India seeing this fixture as an opportunity to fine-tune a couple of loose ends and Ireland entering it hoping to put up a challenge from which passage to the knock-outs becomes easier.While for India this match follows four matches in Australia which they won, for Ireland this is a much bigger fixture. At fourth in Pool B with six points from four matches, Willam Porterfield's team are in a desperate battle with Pakistan and West Indies to secure one of the two remaining qualifying spots. After India, Pakistan remain. It is not, whichever way you look at it, an easy task for Ireland.And yet Porterfield played down the opposition by choosing to focus on what Ireland have done until this point, terming this match "like any other game.""We've gotten into each game with two points up for grabs, and tomorrow isn't any different," he told reporters on Monday. "We've approached each game with great clarity and great professionalism really from our point of view, and we've prepared very well. It's a quicker turn around now from this game than from what we've necessarily had before, but it's a good place as I said before, that we're in, and we've got to start fresh tomorrow and come start time. So like any other game we're going into it looking for a win, and we're looking to make it four out of five tomorrow."Obviously, we've got to try to restrict them with the ball and take wickets. It's just like any game of cricket and in this format the best way of restricting teams is taking those wickets. So we've got to go out there with that mentality throughout the 50 overs of taking wickets. Whatever we do of the first ten overs is going to be big, with the bat or with the ball, we have to start the game well and get into it."Being one point away from the quarter-finals was exciting for Ireland, but their skipper was not getting carried away. "It's a nice position to be in. It's one we want to be in. The quarter-finals is a goal we set ourselves before we came here, so we're in a nice position now, but it doesn't count for anything if we don't keep getting good performances," said Porterfield.After starting their campaign with a four-wicket chase of 305 against West Indies in Nelson, Ireland scraped past UAE by two wickets with four balls in hand, lost to South Africa by 210 runs and pipped Zimbabwe by five runs in a tense match in Hobart two days ago. They will know full well that India will not let them go. The loss to South Africa has dented Ireland's net run-rate (-0.820), and so getting big runs on board becomes imperative.One letdown for Ireland has been Kevin O'Brien, arguably their most high-profile player because of his stunning century that helped beat England in the last World Cup. The big-hitting allrounder has conceded runs at 9.60 and over, and in the narrow loss to Zimbabwe he conceded 90 in ten overs, of which 18 came in the 49th over. The opener and offspinner Paul Stirling has also faded away after he was named Man of the Match in the win over West Indies; he has tallied 22 runs in three innings since that eye-catching 92 and taken just two wickets. Ireland's bowling is their weak link, and the margin of error will be small against India.On a small ground, getting your lengths right will be massive. India's pace attack has performed very well in keeping teams to small totals - only once has an opposition scored more than 200 against them in the tournament - and against Ireland's batsmen, who are used to playing in seam-friendly conditions in the UK, they can be expected to use the same approach.Mohit Sharma, the seamer playing his first World Cup, spoke of the short ball being a method of wickets for him. "If you are a fast bowler, you do tend to bowl bouncers," he told reporters a day ahead of the match. "Yes, there is pace and bounce in these wickets Nobody expects my bouncer to come so fast because it skids as well. That's a plus for me because my bouncer skids a lot which catches the batsmen off guard and as a result there will either a top edge or they will get beaten. I have been hit for fours and sixes also off my bouncers but that's how it goes."India have most bases covered. They have bowled and fielded beyond expectation, Shikhar Dhawan rediscovered his touch, Virat Kohli marked his second World Cup with a fine century and MS Dhoni has led well. MS Dhoni deflected the question of Raina's continued struggles against short-pitched deliveries after the last match by terming it a media blow-up, but Ireland's seam-up attack will test out Raina.
