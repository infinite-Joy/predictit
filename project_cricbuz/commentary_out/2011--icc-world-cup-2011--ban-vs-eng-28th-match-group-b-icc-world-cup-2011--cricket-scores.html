At that point, if asked about the prospects of the England-Bangladesh match at Chittagong, the predictions would have been on the lines of "Bangladesh have a very real possibility of causing an upset." Ten days is a long time in cricket though. Since then, Bangladesh have capitulated against West Indies, and England rose from the dead against South Africa. And suddenly, the words "upset" and "possible" seem a tad more unlikely for Bangladesh.Bangladesh: They came into the World Cup with a mountain of expectation - but the problem was it was a mountain that was neither realistic nor an easy burden to bear. Realistically, a good result for Bangladesh would have been to reach the quarter-finals, but realism and fans expectations don't often co-habit the same planet, with the result that Bangladesh's cricketers were expected to easily make it to the quarters and perhaps even progress further from there. That the weight of unrealistic expectation has affected the team would be understating the case. They were beaten handily by India, managed a win against Ireland in a match that the Associate nation was in control of for large parts and were mauled by the West Indies. With all four established nations - India, South Africa, England and West Indies - considerably ahead of Bangladesh on points and net run rate, this makes their England match a must-win one. The main challenge for their players will be putting the demons of the past behind them before the match. Shakib al Hasan had his house stoned, and that is not the best frame of mind to play such an important cricket match in. Nevertheless, Shakib must lead from the front as he has done often in the past. Bangladesh also need Tamim Iqbal to rediscover the spark in his batting, that has so far been missing in this tournament. He scored runs against India, but at a rather more sedate pace than usual, but his innings against Ireland had more of the explosive Tamim that Bangladesh require. Tamim is the kind of player who can grab the initiative and keep it with his side for extended periods, and Bangladesh need that spark after the lows of last week.England: They lost to Ireland, and seemed set to be swamped by South Africa, and just when it appeared that England were pushing themselves into a corner from which it would be difficult to emerge, their bowlers turned it around for them. The two points against South Africa are invaluable for England after their loss to Ireland. They have suffered injury blows - both Stuart Broad and Kevin Pietersen have returned home - but in Eoin Morgan they have a superb replacement for Pietersen, while Ajmal Shahzad was decent when he replaced Broad against India on a featherbed. England will feel they are on the upswing after that remarkable win, though the upswing was slightly soured by the fact that their coach Andy Flower was not exactly diplomatic in his assessment of Pietersen's injury, saying that "the medical advice was that he could get through the tournament. So we hoped he would bit the bullet and continue." The other problem for England will now be to find an opening partner for Andrew Strauss. Pietersen had looked to be growing into the role, but with him gone, the options are limited. On the plus side, Eoin Morgan's entry means England will have the finisher that they had lacked in earlier matches and their middle-order will be considerably strengthened. England will also want to avoid what they have made their own specialty at this World Cup: making every match a tense, nail-biting affair.
