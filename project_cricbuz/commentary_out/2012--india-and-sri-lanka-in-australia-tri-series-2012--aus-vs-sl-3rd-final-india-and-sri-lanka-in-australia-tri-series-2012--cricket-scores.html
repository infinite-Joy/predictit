Twelve league games plus a best of three finals seemed too lengthy when it started, but the quality of cricket and the nail-biting finishes has sure made the tournament a thourough entertainer. It is all coming to an end though, and aptly, it's a battle of equals as Australia take on Sri Lanka at Adelaide. The two teams have a lot of similarities - their openers are in form, the bowling attacks look slightly weak, and both are plagued by injuries to key players. Sri Lanka: A month back, not many gave Sri Lanka a chance to do well in the tournament. They came into the series after a string of losses which led to changes in leadership including the captain and coach. The first two games did nothing to change that perception either - Sri Lanka competed, but failed. But a thrilling tie with India proved to be a turning point, as the Lankans finished the league stages losing only one more game. The fact that the Sri Lankans have been the best team in the tournament is there for everyone to see - their victories have been convincing, and losses, marginal. The trend continued into the first final as well - they almost made an unbelievable fightback to make what seemed an easy win for Australia a tight finish at Brisbane, and drew level at Adelaide after thrashing Australia with an eight wicket victory. Sri Lanka's big wins have been made possible by their top order, which starts with their captain. Jayawardene, Dilshan, Sangakkara, Chandimal and Thirimanne have been in excellent form and have made runs consistently. They rely a lot on the all-rounders, and despite injury blows to Perera, Maharoof and Angelo Matthews, the replacements seem to be doing a decent job. Tharanga in particular adapted well to his new role in the lower-middle order, which lessens the problems for the visitors. The problems for Sri Lanka have been their bowling. Their lead bowler Lasith Malinga has been inconsistent, but seemed to hit a good rhythm in the previous game. A bowling attack which relies a lot on medium-pace all-rounders, Sri Lanka do seem to miss the likes of Perera and Matthews, but the slow pitch at Adelaide suits their spinners, as was evident in the last game when Dilshan opened the attack. Sri Lanka dropped quite a few catches in that match, and will look for one final disciplined show in the same venue. Australia: Like their opponents, Australia too have a lot of injury worries, which starts from their captain. Michael Clarke rushed his comeback and made a century in the last game, but has been ruled out of the final clash (and the West Indies ODIs) because of a hamstring injury. The other casuality is fast bowler James Pattinson, who has been ruled out with a glute muscle strain. David Warner has a groin strain, but managed to score back to back centuries despite that. He has found good support from the other top order batsmen, but Australia will miss Clarke's batting more than his captaincy. The middle order - led by the Hussey brothers - has been in fine form too, but Australia will expect a bit more from Watson's bat. Australia's bowling attack has a good balance - Lee provides pace, while the likes of McKay, Christian and Watson provide medium pace options.
